## What this brief is for

`ogentic-audit` is positioned as a **court-defensible** audit log. That phrase is doing real work in our marketing, our customer conversations, and the trust pitch we make to regulated industries (healthcare, finance, regtech, AI compliance). This brief lays out:

1. The evidentiary framework in which "court-defensible" has meaning.
2. The specific technical properties of v0.1 that map to that framework.
3. How we compare to the alternatives an auditor or attorney is most likely to know — database audit tables, filesystem attestations, blockchain, systemd-journald, Sigstore Rekor.
4. The known limits and what's genuinely outside our threat model.

If a customer's compliance officer or outside counsel asks "what stops your engineers from rewriting this audit log?" — this brief, plus the [threat model](../security/threat-model.md), is the source we expect them to pull from.

## Evidentiary framework (US-focused)

US federal court is the most-litigated jurisdiction for the kind of fact pattern our customers face (insider tampering, contested AI/system logs, regulated-industry record-keeping). The framework is the **Federal Rules of Evidence**. State courts and non-US jurisdictions have analogous regimes that counsel will need to map into separately.

The four hooks that matter for a tamper-evident audit log:

### 1. Authentication — FRE 901

> "To satisfy the requirement of authenticating … an item of evidence, the proponent must produce evidence sufficient to support a finding that the item is what the proponent claims it is." — FRE 901(a).

For an audit log file, "what it is" generally means *the record of events captured at the time those events occurred, by the system that observed them, attributable to the operator of that system.* Authentication has two parts:

- **Identification** — the file is the file the proponent says it is (no swap, no impersonation).
- **Integrity** — the file's contents are what they were when written (no post-hoc edit).

`ogentic-audit` addresses both:

- *Identification* via the segment header `key_id` field (BLAKE3-256 of the signing key) — the same key signs every record, so the entire log is provably the output of one specific signing context.
- *Integrity* via the HMAC chain — an undetected mid-stream edit would require forging the signing key, not just rewriting bytes.

Counsel will likely care that the foundational testimony to authenticate the log (a custodian / engineer affidavit) ties to *what the system does*, not *what the engineer remembers*. The structured [violation-report.md](../spec/violation-report.md) shape is designed for this — it produces a verifiable, machine-readable verdict that is reproducible by any third-party verifier given the log file and the key.

### 2. Self-authentication — FRE 902

> "The following items of evidence are self-authenticating; they require no extrinsic evidence of authenticity in order to be admitted …" — FRE 902.

FRE 902(13) and 902(14) (added 2017) cover **certified records generated by a process or system** and **certified data copied from electronic devices, storage media, or files**, conditioned on a written certification by a qualified person *meeting the requirements of FRE 902(11) or 902(12)*.

The relevance for `ogentic-audit`: a custodian who can certify (a) the system that generated the records, (b) the methodology used (HMAC-SHA256 chained, append-only, segmented files), and (c) the verification process (CLI / programmatic verifier returning a structured verdict) can offer a self-authenticating bundle. This is materially easier than the traditional FRE 901 path (which requires foundational testimony at trial).

What we ship that supports a 902-style certification:

- **Public, language-agnostic specification** ([`docs/spec/v0.1.md`](../spec/v0.1.md)) — the methodology is not proprietary; an opposing expert can independently verify our claims.
- **Cross-language conformance suite** ([OGE-441](https://linear.app/ogenticai/issue/OGE-441)) — at least two implementations agree on the output bytes for the same input.
- **Reproducible reference vectors** ([`tests/vectors/v0.1/`](../../tests/vectors/v0.1/)) — anyone, including opposing counsel, can verify our verifier's output against shared goldens.
- **Open-source under Apache-2.0** — no vendor-lock-in, no "trust us, the source is closed" objection.

Counsel needs to confirm what specific certification language meets 902(13)/(14) for the customer's jurisdiction and use case.

### 3. Hearsay and the business-records exception — FRE 803(6)

Audit logs are typically offered to prove the truth of what they record (e.g. "user X performed action Y at time T") — that's hearsay. The standard route around hearsay for system-generated records is the **business-records exception**:

> "A record … of an act, event, condition, opinion, or diagnosis [is admissible] if … made at or near the time by — or from information transmitted by — someone with knowledge; … kept in the course of a regularly conducted activity of a business; … making the record was a regular practice of that activity …" — FRE 803(6) (paraphrased).

`ogentic-audit` is well-suited to this exception when the customer's deployment matches the elements:

- *"At or near the time"* — every record carries `ts_wall` (RFC 3339 UTC, ms precision) AND `ts_mono_delta` (monotonic ms since session start). The dual-time-anchor is specifically designed to make "the system recorded this at the time the event happened, not later" a defensible technical claim. The `TimestampInconsistency` violation kind reports any divergence > 60 s.
- *"Regular practice"* — the writer is invoked from the customer's production code path on every relevant event. Whether *that* is a regular practice depends on how the customer integrates; the library doesn't constrain it.
- *"Trustworthiness"* — courts have discretion to exclude business records where "the source of information or the method or circumstances of preparation indicate a lack of trustworthiness." HMAC chains, public spec, and structured violation reports are all evidence of trustworthy method.

### 4. "Tamper-evident" as a legal property

The phrase "tamper-evident" is engineering shorthand. In court it has to translate into something a judge or jury understands. Two formulations that have worked for similar systems (CT logs, git-signed commits, Sigstore Rekor) and we expect to apply here:

- **"You cannot edit a record without leaving a detectable trail."** Concretely: a verifier comparing the on-disk file against the cryptographic chain will report which record was modified, what kind of modification it was, and at what byte offset. The structured violation report ([violation-report.md](../spec/violation-report.md)) is the exhibit that demonstrates this.
- **"Detection does not require trusting the operator."** A third party with the public key and the spec can verify without our involvement. This is the load-bearing claim for "court-defensible" — the credibility of the verdict does not depend on us.

These map to legal arguments about chain of custody and integrity-on-its-face that counsel will recognize.

## What `ogentic-audit` v0.1 specifically does

For each technical property below, the legal "so what?" is stated explicitly so counsel doesn't have to translate.

### HMAC-SHA256 chain over canonical CBOR

Every record's `payload_bytes` is HMAC'd with the writer's signing key. Each record carries its predecessor's HMAC in `prev_hash`, signed as part of its own payload. The chain root is bound to the segment header (`HMAC(key, header_bytes[0..72])`).

**Legal so-what:** an attacker who modifies any byte of any record breaks the chain. The verifier reports the exact failure (kind, segment_index, record_id, byte offset, expected vs. actual hex). Without the signing key, no attacker can repair the chain. The strength of the cryptographic claim is HMAC-SHA256's collision resistance — an actively-researched, IETF-standard primitive used by banking, TLS, and federal cryptographic standards (FIPS 198-1).

### Append-only at the filesystem level

The writer never rewrites existing record bytes. The only rewrite operation is post-crash truncation of a torn last record (the recovery primitive: `len_trailer == len_prefix`). This is a stronger claim than "append-only API on top of a database," because it doesn't rely on the database's internals not rewriting pages.

**Legal so-what:** an opposing expert auditing a forensic disk image can verify, byte-for-byte, that no record was rewritten in place. The filesystem-level append-only property removes a category of "yes but the storage layer rewrites things" objections that arise with SQLite, journaled databases, and similar.

### Dual-time-anchor (wall + monotonic + session_id)

Every record carries `ts_wall` (UTC), `ts_mono_delta` (ms since session start, monotonic clock), and `session_id` (UUIDv4 generated at vault unlock). The verifier reports `TimestampInconsistency` if `|wall_delta - mono_delta| > 60 s` within a session.

**Legal so-what:** clock-tampering is a known forensic objection. To forge a backdated record, an attacker has to forge **two clocks that disagreed in correlated fashion** at the time of the alleged event, plus a self-consistent `session_id`, plus re-sign every subsequent record. Each requirement compounds the difficulty. Compare this to a single-source timestamp (NTP-synced wall clock only), which can be moved by changing one OS setting.

### Structured violation reports

The verifier emits machine-readable JSON ([`docs/spec/violation-report.md`](../spec/violation-report.md)) with one of nine documented violation kinds, the segment_index + record_id + byte offset of the failure, and the cryptographic evidence (expected vs. actual hex). The shape is normative — every conforming implementation produces the same shape.

**Legal so-what:** the verdict an auditor or attorney sees is not narrative ("looks tampered to me") but exhibit-grade — a JSON document with reproducible fields. Hand the verifier output to an opposing expert and they get the same verdict; that's the property courts want from technical evidence.

### CLI export to court-ready PDF (C3 / [OGE-438](https://linear.app/ogenticai/issue/OGE-438), v0.1 milestone)

The CLI's `export --pdf` subcommand will produce a self-contained PDF for hand-off to counsel: chain head, integrity verdict, key-id provenance, generation timestamp, and a record listing. This is the artifact that goes into a discovery production or a regulator response.

**Legal so-what:** the workflow an attorney or compliance officer expects is "give me a document I can put in front of a judge." Not a binary file plus a CLI invocation. The PDF is the courtroom artifact; the underlying chain remains the source of truth.

## Comparison to alternatives

What an opposing expert is most likely to know — and why we think we're better positioned for the courtroom than each.

### Database audit tables (Postgres `pgaudit`, MySQL audit plugin, etc.)

- **Mutable.** A DBA with `UPDATE` privilege on the audit table can edit history. This is a real attack and a known forensic weakness.
- **Vendor-trust.** Defending the integrity of a Postgres audit log requires defending Postgres's internals — page rewrites, WAL checkpointing, vacuums.
- **Where they win:** transactional consistency with the data they audit. Nothing in `ogentic-audit` does that; if the customer needs "the audit row and the data row commit atomically," they need the DB-internal approach (and accept its trade-offs).

### Filesystem attestations (TPM-backed, IMA, Apple Endpoint Security)

- **Snapshot-only.** Attests "this file existed at this time with this hash." Does not chain — once the file changes, the prior attestation is informational.
- **Where they win:** boot-time integrity and platform-level guarantees we don't try to provide.

### Blockchain-anchored audit logs

- **Overkill.** A consensus protocol designed for adversarial multi-party settings; we have a single-user vault on a single device.
- **Cost.** Real money per-record at any meaningful chain.
- **Court status.** Mixed. Public-blockchain anchoring has been admitted in some cases for hash-of-a-document attestation, but the chain itself is rarely the load-bearing evidence.
- **Where they win:** "even if you and the customer are both compromised, an external witness anchored the chain head at time T." This is a v0.2+ direction (see ADR-0001 § Future direction) but out of v0.1 scope.

### `systemd-journald` (forward-secure signing)

- **Forward-secure.** Uses an FSPRG so even if the current signing key is compromised, the attacker cannot forge prior records. Genuinely interesting prior art and explicitly reserved as a v0.2 direction in [ADR-0001](../adr/0001-on-disk-format.md).
- **Court status.** Limited precedent. Used in production by many Linux deployments but rarely cited as the load-bearing evidence in litigation.
- **Where they win:** the FSPRG property. v0.2 may bring this in.

### Sigstore Rekor / Trillian / Certificate Transparency

- **Public-witness model.** A public log with adversarial gossip; signed tree heads. Strongest court-defensibility profile in OSS today, and the closest peer for our positioning.
- **Different threat model.** Designed for many parties contributing to one log, where no party trusts any other. We are single-user, single-device.
- **Where they win:** "subrange proofs" (prove this record was in the log at time T without disclosing the rest of the log) and "split-view detection" (prove the log operator didn't show a different log to different parties). We don't need either at v0.1.
- **Why we still differ:** Trillian's machinery (Merkle trees, append-only DB, tree heads) is heavyweight for our threat model. ADR-0001 explicitly considers and rejects E (Merkle) for v0.1; the rejection is documented and reversible at v0.2 if multi-tenant emerges.

## Limits and caveats

These are stated bluntly so counsel knows where the line is and so we don't oversell.

- **Single-user vault threat model.** v0.1 assumes the audit log lives inside one user's encrypted vault on one device. Multi-tenant deployments (server-side, shared-key) are out of scope and are tracked under [OGE-460](https://linear.app/ogenticai/issue/OGE-460) and the v0.2 server-side roadmap.
- **HMAC-key-compromise = vault-passphrase-compromise.** Both derive from the same Argon2id root in the consumer's deployment model. An attacker with the vault passphrase has the HMAC key; from that point forward they can forge records that verify. The threat model documents this and reserves forward-secure signing (FSPRG) for v0.2.
- **Compromised-process residual risk.** If an attacker has live read access to the running writer's memory, they have the signing key. v0.1 does not defend against this; it is documented as accepted residual risk in the [threat model](../security/threat-model.md).
- **Jurisdiction.** This brief is US-focused (FRE-based). Customers in EU, UK, Canada, Australia, or other jurisdictions need a separate analysis from local counsel. The technical predicates above are jurisdiction-independent; the evidentiary framework is not.
- **Counsel review required.** Specific applicability to a specific case must be reviewed by counsel. Engineering has not validated, and cannot validate, claims about admissibility in any specific proceeding.
- **Pre-1.0 stability.** The on-disk format is locked at `v0.1` per [the spec](../spec/v0.1.md), but the library is pre-1.0 and the API surface may change. A log written by a v0.1.0 release will be readable by every v0.1.x release and by any future release that supports format `v0.1`; that's the durability promise.

## Server-side / KMS-backed deployments

The analysis above applies to single-user desktop deployments.  This section
extends it to server-side deployments that use AWS KMS `GenerateMac` as the
signing primitive (see `docs/adr/0002-server-side-kms-key-sourcing.md` and
`docs/integrations/server-side-kms.md`).

### CloudTrail as a parallel chain-of-custody artefact

Every `GenerateMac` call is logged by AWS CloudTrail with:

- **Timestamp** — UTC, millisecond precision.
- **IAM principal** — the identity that made the request (role, user, or
  assumed-role session).
- **Key ID** — the KMS key ARN (the managed key identifier, not the HMAC output).
- **AWS Request ID** — a unique identifier for the API call.

This produces a parallel chain-of-custody record alongside the audit log itself.
A custodian can attest: "This MAC was produced by IAM principal `arn:aws:iam::...`
at this time under key `arn:aws:kms:...`, as evidenced by the attached CloudTrail
record."

Paired with the audit log's own HMAC chain, the combined artefact supports a
strengthened FRE 902(13)/(14) self-authentication argument: the custodian can
certify both the HMAC chain (the audit log's internal integrity) and the
CloudTrail record (the external evidence of which principal signed what and when).

### Implications for the FRE 902 certification

The existing FRE 902(13)/(14) path (see §2 above) required a certification that
the system produced the records in the ordinary course of its operation.  For KMS
deployments, the certification now spans two systems:

1. The audit library (`ogentic-audit-core` + `ogentic-audit-kms`) — which produced
   the on-disk log file.
2. AWS KMS + CloudTrail — which produced the parallel key-use log.

Counsel must confirm that the certification language covers both sources.  The
CloudTrail log lives in AWS-controlled storage with its own retention semantics
(default 90 days for Management Events; configurable via S3 lifecycle policies).
Operators must ensure CloudTrail is retained for at least as long as the audit log
itself may be subpoenaed.

### Concrete caveat: chain-of-custody spans two systems

The v0.1 desktop threat model permitted a "single-party, single-device" chain of
custody: the user's laptop holds the log and the key.  In KMS deployments, the
chain of custody now spans:

- The log files (operator-controlled storage).
- The CloudTrail log (AWS-controlled storage, S3 bucket in the operator's account).

An opposing expert will scrutinise both.  If CloudTrail records are missing
(retention expired, trail disabled, bucket policy overridden), the parallel
chain-of-custody claim weakens to the audit log's internal HMAC chain alone,
which is still valid but loses the external-witness component.

**Recommendation:** Enable CloudTrail with a dedicated S3 bucket for audit key
usage events, with Object Lock (WORM) and a lifecycle policy matching your
document retention requirements.  Document this in your certification of process.

### What AWS KMS adds and does not add

**Adds:**

- HSM-grade key residency — key material never in process memory.
- IAM scoping — per-key, per-action, per-principal access control.
- CloudTrail audit of every signing event — the parallel chain-of-custody artefact.

**Does not add:**

- Protection from an attacker who has gained `kms:GenerateMac`-capable IAM
  credentials.  An adversary with valid IAM credentials for the audit key can forge
  records that verify, just as a desktop attacker with the HMAC key can.  The
  recommended mitigation is tight IAM scoping:
  one key, one action, condition on `kms:MacAlgorithm = HMAC_SHA_256`, no wildcards.
- Protection from the KMS service itself — operators must trust AWS as a custodian
  for the key residency claim.

**Legal so-what:** the court-defensibility argument for KMS deployments adds a
layer of external attestation (CloudTrail) that the desktop deployment lacks.  It
also adds an external trust dependency (AWS).  Counsel should weigh both sides
when advising on the certification of process.
